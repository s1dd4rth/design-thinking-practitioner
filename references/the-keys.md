# The Keys — Hills, Playbacks, Sponsor Users

The Keys are the three techniques that keep diverse teams aligned as they move from idea to outcome. They get teams aligned (Hills), keep them aligned across time (Playbacks), and keep them aligned with reality (Sponsor Users).

---

## HILLS — statements of intent as user outcomes
A Hill tells the team *where to go, not how to get there*, so they can find breakthrough solutions without losing the goal. A good Hill is **implementation-independent** — if it names a specific tool or feature, rewrite it.

### Anatomy: Who / What / Wow
- **Who** — the specific user you serve (and, by implication, who you don't).
- **What** — the need/outcome they're trying to achieve (a goal, not a feature).
- **Wow** — the differentiator: how you beat the alternative, and how you'll measure success.

**Template:** *A [Who] can [What] [Wow — the differentiating measure].*

### Worked examples (use as calibration, don't reproduce as the user's answer)
- A sales leader can assemble a response team in under 24 hours without management involvement. *(Who: sales leader · What: assemble a team · Wow: <24h, no management)*
- A developer can build and run an app using first- and third-party APIs in under 30 minutes.
- JFK's moon-shot framing — a bold, measurable, implementation-independent outcome — is the archetype.

### Facilitating a Hill (run it like this)
1. Start with the **Who**. Force specificity — "developers" is weaker than "a developer new to our platform."
2. Draw out the **What** as an outcome. If they say a feature, ask "and what does that let the user *do*?"
3. Find the **Wow** — the measurable differentiator. "How would we know we nailed this? What's the number or the unfair advantage?"
4. Read it back. If it smells like an implementation, take another pass.
5. **Pressure-test:** Is it implementation-independent? Is it a genuine user outcome? Is it measurable? Is it feasible within constraints?

### Rules of thumb
- **No more than three Hills** at a time; reserve a slice of capacity for the **Foundation** (fixing past issues / future groundwork).
- Hills evolve — don't demand perfection on day one. They can change right up to **Playback Zero**, then commit.
- Ownership: product teams → product management (with design + engineering); service teams → senior client stakeholder (with the delivery team).
- A complex Hill can be split into **Sub-Hills**, but each must still deliver standalone user value.

## PLAYBACKS — align stakeholders across time through story
A Playback brings stakeholders into the Loop in a safe space to **tell stories and exchange feedback**. It reveals alignment or misalignment and measures progress against the big-picture problem. A Playback is a *story*, not a status report — users are the stars; give them a name and a face.

### Anatomy (three beats)
1. **Invite stakeholders** — anyone the work affects; err toward inclusivity, across silos and hierarchy.
2. **Tell your story** — characters, relationships, plot, stakes. "Features and requirements are forgotten; stories endure."
3. **Listen for feedback and misalignment** — capture without judgment; good feedback can come from anyone. Misalignment isn't failure — it means take another loop.

### Milestone sequence (typical software team)
- **Hills Playback** — earliest. Agree on intended outcomes, Foundation, and resource allocation. Hold draft playbacks ("Playback -2, -1") to iterate toward alignment.
- **Playback Zero** — commit to what will actually be delivered. Tell a complete, realistic user journey per Hill at mid-fidelity; "travel through" it the way a user would. After this, break into epics/stories and start production code.
- **Delivery Playbacks** — at sprint ends, using the *real working solution*. Tell the end-to-end story (not a feature demo) to find UX gaps.
- **Client Playbacks** — with NDA'd clients; share roadmap, the three Hills, and intended experience; gather feedback.

### Facilitating a Playback
- Help the team build the **narrative**, not a slide list: who's the user, what's their day, where does it hurt, how does the solution change the story.
- Decide **fidelity** by milestone (sketches early, mid-fi at Zero, working software at delivery).
- Pre-build a **feedback-capture** structure so input is recorded neutrally.
- Treat outcome honestly: aligned → move forward; misaligned → loop again, no panic.

## SPONSOR USERS — align with real-world reality
Sponsor Users are real (or potential) users who regularly contribute domain expertise as **active participants**, not passive subjects. They break the empathy barrier — but don't fully replace formal research/usability studies.

### Selection criteria (all three must hold)
1. **Representative** of the *actual* target user — not the client/buyer, not an extreme outlier (a race-car driver is wrong for a family-minivan Hill).
2. **Personally invested** in the outcome — ideally a demanding (not extreme) use case, so they have a stake in your success.
3. **Available** to collaborate regularly — a real commitment, though not full-time; respect and flex to their schedule.

### How to work with them
- Recruit **at least one Sponsor User per Hill**; write Hills and know the target user *before* recruiting.
- **Observe** through their eyes; **Reflect** with them (include them in Playbacks, let them help refine Hills); **Make** collaboratively (give them tools to contribute ideas).
- Treat them as part of the team — they don't always get their way, but if they say you're not solving their problem or you're adding complexity, believe them.
- Ownership: product teams → product management + design (sales/marketing source candidates); service teams → the client sources candidates, but the product team defines the criteria.

### Facilitating Sponsor User recruitment
Produce a **Sponsor User profile** per Hill: target-user description, the demanding use case that signals investment, the collaboration cadence/expectations, and where to source candidates. Then a short outreach/criteria brief the team can act on.
